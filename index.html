Why

Design heuristics are guidelines to facilitate UX/UI/Visual Design discussions and reviews. They offer successful principles based on experience and experimentation in which to critique a design. The key is the discussion around designs should be based on goals and not opinions. For example saying “I dislike red” is unhelpful. Whereas stating “I don’t think the red works because it is usually used to communicated error messages” is a more valid and constructive assessment. The following heuristics provide vocabulary in which to aid these critical discussions.

Findability

Are the key elements and major pathways, tasks and goals easily located? Can the user find it where they expect or want to find it?

Clear

Is the design easy to understand on the first encounter? Is it simple, succinct, and the path to complete the task obvious? This heuristic includes messaging and text, not just the user interface and visual design.

Controllable

Are features offered to allow the user to tailor information or functionality to their context?

Learnable

Is the design easy to understand after the first interaction? This heuristic pertains to actions that are repeatable and are predictable after the first use.

Consistency

Is there a visual language that creates a cohesive experience for the user? When things always behave the same, users don’t have to worry about what will happen. Instead, they know what will happen based on earlier experience.

Credible

Is the design worthy of confidence and instill trust & reliability in the user?

Communicative

Does the user always know where they are? Are there elements that orient he/she in the UI or text that are clear across pages, contexts and tasks?

Valuable

Is the design of great use, service and importance to the user?

Delight

Does the design exceed user expectations? This heuristic involves including an unexpected element that the user finds entertaining.
